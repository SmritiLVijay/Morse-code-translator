What is morse code?

Morse code is a system that uses short and long signals, called dots and dashes, to represent alphabet, numerals, and punctuation marks. Samuel Morse, an American scientist, helped developed it to send messages over long distances using wires, radios, sound, or light. Messages are sent by tapping, drumming, buzzing, clicking, or flashing light. At the time of its invention, it was the fastest long-distance form of communication through telegraphs.

International Morse code is composed of five elements:

  • short mark, dot or dit ( ▄ ): "dit duration" is one time unit long
  • long mark, dash or dah ( ▄▄▄ ): three time units long
  • inter-element gap between the dits and dahs within a character: one dot duration or one unit long
  • short gap (between letters): three time units long
  • medium gap (between words): seven time units long

    • How to use?

    • In written morse, dots or dits(.) and dashes or dahs(-) are used.
    • After every letter, leave a space.
    • After every word, leave seven spaces or use a /(forward slash).
